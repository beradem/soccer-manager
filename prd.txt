## Reference Game: Basketball GM (ZenGM) — Deep Research

This section documents Basketball GM as a primary reference and inspiration for what we will build with Soccer GM. Basketball GM is the closest analogue to our vision: a free, browser-based, single-player sports management simulation game. Understanding its design, features, and architecture informs every major decision we make.

---

### 1. What Is Basketball GM?

Basketball GM is a completely free, single-player basketball management simulation game playable entirely in the browser, developed and maintained by ZenGM (an independent game developer). Players take the role of a general manager (GM) of a basketball franchise, tasked with building a championship-winning roster through drafts, trades, free agency signings, and roster management — all while staying within a salary cap and satisfying the team owner.

Key facts:
- 100% free, no microtransactions, no virtual currency, no pay-to-win mechanics
- No account required to play
- Unlimited leagues and seasons
- Runs entirely client-side (no server-side game logic)
- As of the most recent annual report: 7 million leagues created, 25 million seasons simulated
- Sister games on the same engine: Football GM, ZenGM Baseball, ZenGM Hockey

**Why this matters for Soccer GM:** This is the exact product-market fit we are targeting — a free, frictionless, deep management sim for soccer fans.

---

### 2. Core Gameplay Loop

The game is structured around a repeating seasonal cycle:

1. **Preseason** — Players age and develop. Young players improve, older players decline. Coaches adjust lineups.
2. **Regular Season** — Games are simulated. The player controls pacing (simulate 1 game, 1 week, until playoffs, etc.) via a Play Menu.
3. **Playoffs** — Top 8 teams per conference enter a bracket. Best-of-7 series until a champion is crowned.
4. **Draft** — Teams pick incoming prospects in reverse-standings order (worst teams pick first), two rounds.
5. **Free Agency** — Re-sign your own players, then open market begins. Salary cap governs what you can afford.
6. **Repeat** — Each season compounds on the last; player careers, retirements, and dynasty-building span many seasons.

The Play Menu is the central UI control — a single button that drives the player through each phase of the season, with options to stop at any meaningful decision point.

**Soccer GM analog:** We mirror this loop exactly, replacing basketball phases with soccer equivalents (preseason, league season, cup competitions, transfer windows, promotion/relegation).

---

### 3. Roster Management

- Maximum roster size: 15 players (minimum: 5 to play games)
- Players have individual ratings (0–100 scale) across multiple attributes: shooting, rebounding, passing, dribbling, defense, athleticism, etc.
- Overall rating is a composite, but raw attribute mix matters — a player's teammates and team system affect output
- Roster page shows how each player was acquired (drafted, traded, free agent signed) via an "Acquired" column
- Players can be released, but guaranteed contracts still count against the cap

**Soccer GM analog:** We will manage squads of 25, with positions (GK, CB, LB, RB, CM, CAM, ST, etc.) and attribute ratings (pace, dribbling, passing, shooting, defending, physical). A "How acquired" column is similarly useful for immersion.

---

### 4. Player Ratings & Scouting

- Player ratings are hidden behind a **scouting system** — what the GM sees are estimates, not true values
- Higher scouting budget → more accurate ratings (effect is gradual, takes ~3 seasons to fully reflect)
- Ratings displayed on a 0–100 scale
- Special attributes: Offensive IQ (OIQ) and Defensive IQ (DIQ) replace simpler block/steal ratings
- Potential (POT) is a hidden rating indicating a player's development ceiling

**Soccer GM analog:** Scouting is a core system in soccer management games. We will implement a similar fog-of-war on ratings for unknown players, with scouting spend improving accuracy over time. Youth players will have high potential but uncertain ratings.

---

### 5. Salary Cap & Contracts

Basketball GM models a nuanced salary cap system:

- **Default cap:** $90M (soft cap)
- **Luxury tax threshold:** $100M — exceeding it triggers a 150% tax penalty on the overage
- **Soft cap rules:** Teams can exceed the cap to re-sign their own players, but cannot exceed it to sign free agents from other teams
- **Hard cap mode** (optional): No exceptions — you cannot exceed the cap to re-sign or trade
- Contracts are fully guaranteed for non-rookies; releasing a player still costs cap space
- Rookie contracts are not guaranteed until the regular season starts (releasing before then costs nothing)
- Player mood traits influence whether they'll negotiate and what they demand (affected by team success, facilities, city size, difficulty)

**Soccer GM analog:** Soccer uses wage budgets and transfer fees rather than a salary cap. We will implement a weekly wage budget and transfer budget (separate pools), with Financial Fair Play rules as an optional hard constraint mode. Contract length, release clauses, and agent fees all add depth.

### 6. Draft System

- Two-round draft modeled after the NBA
- Order based on regular season + playoff performance (worst teams pick first)
- No lottery by default, but configurable
- Undrafted players enter free agency
- Settings for draft prospect age ranges and forced retirement age

**Soccer GM analog:** We will implement a youth academy / scouting system for developing homegrown players, plus a transfer market (the soccer equivalent of free agency and trades). Transfer windows (January + Summer) replace the single draft event.

**Soccer GM analog:** Transfer negotiations in Soccer GM will be the equivalent of trades, with bid/offer mechanics, player valuation, and AI clubs that have their own needs and budgets.

### 8. Player Development & Aging

- Young players tend to improve; veterans plateau and eventually decline
- Injuries have lasting effects: serious injuries (25+ games) can permanently reduce athleticism ratings
- Injury risk increases with age
- Real player determinism (RPD) setting: historical real players follow their real-world development arcs

**Soccer GM analog:** Development curves will follow realistic soccer career trajectories (peak at 26–29, physical decline earlier). Youth player development will be a key system — investing in youngsters vs. buying proven talent is a central strategic decision.

---

### 9. Finances & Team Management

- Team finances include player salaries, scouting budget, coaching budget, facilities budget, and health/training budget
- Scouting budget directly affects how accurately the GM sees player ratings
- Owner sends an annual performance evaluation; poor performance leads to firing

**Soccer GM analog:** We will track club finances: wage bill, transfer spend/income, matchday revenue, prize money, and commercial income. Board expectations (win ratio, cup runs, youth development) determine job security.

**Soccer GM analog:** We will generate full match stats (goals, shots, passes, possession, cards, etc.) per game with optional play-by-play. Leagues will use standard soccer formats: round-robin group stage + knockout rounds for cups; promotion/relegation for leagues.

## Deep Research: How Basketball GM's Game Simulation Engine Works

This section documents the technical internals of Basketball GM's game simulation engine in detail, based on analysis of the open source code at `src/worker/core/GameSim.basketball/index.ts` and related files. This serves as the primary technical reference for designing Soccer GM's own match engine.

---

### Overview: What Kind of Simulation Is It?

Basketball GM uses a **possession-by-possession, play-by-play simulation**. Every single possession in every game is individually resolved using probability calculations derived from player ratings. This is the highest-fidelity approach to sports simulation — as opposed to a "box score sim" that just rolls dice for totals — and produces realistic play-by-play logs, accurate per-player stat distribution, and emergent game narratives (comebacks, hot streaks, foul trouble, clutch moments).

The simulation runs inside a **Shared Worker** (background thread), keeping the UI fully responsive even while computing thousands of possessions per second. In 2017, "Project Iverson" achieved a 10x performance improvement in simulation speed through algorithmic and architecture optimizations, enabling fast-forward through entire seasons in seconds.

---

### Player Ratings: The Foundation

Every player has **15 core atomic ratings** on a 0–100 scale:

| Rating Key | Full Name | What It Governs |
|---|---|---|
| `hgt` | Height | Blocking, rebounding, interior scoring, defense |
| `stre` | Strength | Defense, rebounding, low-post scoring |
| `spd` | Speed | Ball handling, fast breaks, perimeter defense |
| `jmp` | Jumping | Finishing at rim, rebounding, shot blocking |
| `endu` | Endurance | How quickly fatigue degrades all other ratings |
| `ins` | Inside Scoring | Low-post and interior shot accuracy |
| `dnk` | Dunking | At-rim finishing and dunk frequency |
| `ft` | Free Throw | Free throw accuracy |
| `fg` | Mid-Range FG | Mid-range two-point accuracy |
| `tp` | Three-Point | Three-point shot accuracy |
| `oiq` | Offensive IQ | Decision-making, usage, shot selection |
| `diq` | Defensive IQ | Defensive positioning, help defense |
| `drb` | Dribbling | Ball handling, turnover avoidance |
| `pss` | Passing | Assist generation, turnover avoidance |
| `reb` | Rebounding | Both offensive and defensive rebounding |

A critical design insight: **OIQ and DIQ are multipliers, not additive stats.** Young players almost universally have low OIQ/DIQ — raw talent exists but lacks the intelligence to leverage it. As players age and gain experience, improvement comes primarily from basketball IQ amplifying their existing physical skills. This models real-world athletic development realistically.

Another key design choice: ratings are bounded 0–100 but the full range is commonly used across the population of players. Early builds had rating inflation issues (e.g., 5 players all with 100 three-point rating simultaneously). The solution was calibrating distributions so ratings mean something relative to the historical distribution of real NBA players.

---

### Composite Ratings: From Atoms to Simulation Inputs

Raw ratings are never used directly in simulation calculations. Instead, they are combined into **composite ratings** — weighted linear combinations of multiple atomic ratings, normalized to a 0–1 scale. A `power` parameter can then be applied for nonlinear scaling.

Key composite ratings used in simulation:

| Composite | Description | Component Ratings (example weights) |
|---|---|---|
| `pace` | How fast a team pushes the tempo | `spd`, `jmp`, `dnk`, `tp`, `drb`, `pss` |
| `usage` | Tendency to be the primary ball-handler | `ins`, `dnk`, `fg`, `tp`, `spd`, `hgt`, `drb`, `oiq` |
| `dribbling` | Ball-handling ability | `drb`, `oiq` |
| `passing` | Playmaking ability | `pss`, `oiq` |
| `shootingAtRim` | Finishing near the basket | `hgt`, `stre`, `spd`, `jmp`, `ins`, `dnk`, `oiq` |
| `shootingLowPost` | Low-post scoring | `hgt`, `stre`, `jmp`, `ins`, `oiq` |
| `shootingMidRange` | Mid-range jump shots | `fg`, `oiq` |
| `shootingThreePointer` | Three-point shooting | `tp`, `oiq` (and `-oiq` penalty for poor shooters) |
| `shootingFt` | Free throw accuracy | `ft`, `oiq` |
| `rebounding` | Securing missed shots | `hgt`, `stre`, `jmp`, `endu`, `reb`, `oiq` |
| `turnovers` | Tendency to turn the ball over | `drb`, `pss`, `oiq` (inverted — higher = better) |
| `stealing` | Ability to generate steals | `spd`, `diq` |
| `blocking` | Shot blocking ability | `hgt`, `jmp`, `diq` |
| `defensePrimeter` | Perimeter defensive ability | `spd`, `jmp`, `diq` |
| `defenseInterior` | Interior defensive ability | `hgt`, `stre`, `jmp`, `diq` |
| `endurance` | Stamina and fatigue resistance | `endu`, `stre` |

The conversion formula for each composite rating is roughly:
```
compositeRating = clamp(sum(weight_i × rating_i / 100) / sum(weights), 0, 1) ^ power
```

The `power` parameter allows ratings to cluster in the middle (power < 1) or spread toward extremes (power > 1). An inverted composite (power = -1) converts a "bad" quality into its inverse.

---

### Team Composite Ratings: The Five-Man Unit

Simulation doesn't use individual player composites directly — it uses **team composite ratings** computed from all five players currently on the court. These are updated **once per possession**, after substitutions and synergy are recalculated.

Formula:
```
teamRating[type] = (Σ over 5 players: compositeRating[type] × fatigueMultiplier × performanceFactor × foulFactor) / 5
                 + synergyBonus[type]
```

Each of the four multiplicative adjustments:

1. **`fatigueMultiplier`**: Scales 0–1 based on player energy. A fully fresh player has multiplier 1.0; an exhausted player drops toward ~0.6–0.7.

2. **`performanceFactor`**: A `tanh`-based penalty for trailing teams:
   ```
   performanceFactor = 1 - 0.2 × tanh(scoreDiff / 60)
   ```
   A team down by 30 runs at ~85% effectiveness. This models the psychological and tactical reality that large deficits demoralize teams.

3. **`foulFactor`**: Players in deep foul trouble (e.g., 4 fouls in a game) have their contribution reduced (factor 0.1–1.0). This also increases the likelihood they get subbed out.

4. **`synergyBonus`**: A flat additive bonus from team skill combinations (see Synergy section below).

---

### Synergy: Team-Level Skill Combinations

Beyond individual player composite ratings, Basketball GM models **team synergy** — the idea that certain skill combinations at the team level unlock additional performance bonuses.

Synergy is calculated by counting the "presence" of each skill across all five players on court using sigmoid-shaped threshold functions:
```
skillPresence = sigmoid(compositeRating - threshold) × weight
teamSynergy[skill] = sum(skillPresence across 5 players), capped at 1.0
```

**Offensive synergy categories:**
- Three-point shooting (rewards teams with multiple good shooters — spacing effect)
- Dribble-drive creation (ball handlers that generate penetration)
- Passing/playmaking (multiple passers create ball movement)
- Low-post presence

**Defensive synergy categories:**
- Perimeter defense (multiple good perimeter defenders)
- Interior defense / shot blocking
- Athleticism / help defense rotation speed

**Rebounding synergy:**
- Teams with multiple rebounders compound the advantage

Each synergy type adds a small bonus (typically +0.01–0.05) to the corresponding team composite rating. This incentivizes building balanced rosters with complementary skills rather than stacking one archetype.

---

### The Main Simulation Loop

At the top level, the simulation follows this structure:

```
simGame():
  initialize()           // Set lineups, jump ball for possession, compute initial synergy
  simRegulation()        // Run 4 quarters
  if tied: simOvertime() // Run overtime periods until resolved
  return gameResult      // Full box score + play-by-play log
```

Within each period:
```
simPeriod():
  while clock > 0 and game not over:
    simPossession()
    checkEndOfPeriod()
    if dead ball: checkSubstitutions()
    checkElamEnding()   // Special target-score ending mode
```

**Key detail:** Team assignments inside `simPossession` are swapped at the top of each iteration (offense ↔ defense), meaning the possession that was just played's defense becomes the new offense. This is a coding convention to simplify state management.

---

### Possession Resolution: `getPossessionOutcome()`

This is the heart of the simulation. Each possession ends in exactly one of these outcomes:

1. **Turnover (no steal)** — Offensive team commits a bad pass/handling error, defense recovers
2. **Turnover (steal)** — Defense actively intercepts; may trigger fast break
3. **Shot attempt** — Leads to:
   - Made field goal (2 or 3 points)
   - Made field goal + and-one foul opportunity
   - Missed shot → rebound resolution
   - Blocked shot → rebound resolution
4. **Non-shooting foul** → Free throws

Resolution proceeds through a decision tree of probability checks:

#### Step 1: Turnover Check

```
probTov = turnoverFactor × (0.14 × teamDefenseRating) / (0.5 × (teamDribbling + teamPassing))
```

If `random() < probTov`, possession ends in turnover. Then check for steal:
```
probStl = stealFactor × (0.45 × teamDefensePerimeter) / (0.5 × (teamDribbling + teamPassing))
```
Steal rate affects whether the defense gets a fast break opportunity on the next possession.

#### Step 2: Shot Selection

If no turnover, a shot is attempted. The simulation first selects **which player shoots** using weighted random selection:
```
weights[player] = compositeRating.usage[player] × fatigueMultiplier[player]
shooterIndex = weightedRandomSelect(weights)
```

Then selects **shot type** based on the shooter's relative strengths:
```
atRimWeight = compositeRating.shootingAtRim
lowPostWeight = compositeRating.shootingLowPost
midRangeWeight = compositeRating.shootingMidRange
threeWeight = compositeRating.shootingThreePointer
```
Shot type chosen by weighted random among the four types. This produces realistic shot distributions: a center takes mostly at-rim and low-post shots; a shooter takes mostly threes.

#### Step 3: Block Check

If a shot is attempted:
```
probBlk = blockFactor × 0.2 × defenderBlockingRating²
```
If blocked, the possession continues to rebound resolution (either defense secures it or offense tips it in).

#### Step 4: Shot Accuracy

Base accuracy per shot type (approximate values from source):
- **At rim**: 0.45–0.68 (depends heavily on athleticism and interior defense)
- **Low post**: 0.38–0.58
- **Mid-range**: 0.35–0.52
- **Three-pointer**: 0.28–0.44

Actual make probability per possession:
```
probMake = baseRate - (defenseWeight × teamDefenseRating) + synergyOffensiveBonus
probMake × fatigueMultiplier
```

#### Step 5: And-One / Foul Check

If shot made:
```
probAndOne = andOneFactor[shotType]  // ~0.01 for threes, ~0.15 for at-rim
```
If shot missed:
```
probFoulOnMiss = foulFactor[shotType]  // triggers free throw opportunity
```

#### Step 6: Rebound Resolution

After a missed (and non-fouled) shot:
```
probDrb = (0.75 × (2 + teamDefRebounding)) / (orbFactor × (2 + teamOffRebounding))
```
If `random() < probDrb`, defense secures the rebound (possession ends). Otherwise, offense retains possession for a put-back opportunity.

The individual rebounder is selected by weighted random:
```
weights[player] = compositeRating.rebounding × fatigueMultiplier
```

---

### Fatigue: Energy Depletion and Recovery

Every player on court continuously loses energy:
```
energy -= minutesPlayedThisPossession × 0.055 × (1 - compositeRating.endurance)
```

Energy recovers on the bench:
```
energy += minutesBenched × 0.094 × (1 - compositeRating.endurance)
```
(Higher endurance rating → slower drain, faster recovery)

Energy converts to a fatigue multiplier applied to all composite ratings:
```
fatigueMultiplier = fatigue(energy)  // Roughly linear from 0.6 (exhausted) to 1.0 (fresh)
```

**Fatigue impact is reduced in late-game situations** (final 6 minutes of regulation, final 2 minutes of overtime). This reflects real-world adrenaline effects and prevents unrealistic late-game collapses from pure exhaustion.

**Fatigue also drives substitution logic:** The auto-substitution system evaluates player energy levels and foul trouble each dead ball and swaps in fresher players when appropriate. Coaches (the AI) try to distribute minutes to keep key players fresh for crunch time.

---

### Foul System

Fouls are tracked per player, per quarter, and per team. Key mechanics:

- **Team foul bonus**: After N team fouls in a quarter (default: 5), every subsequent defensive foul sends the offensive player to the line
- **Personal foul trouble**: A player approaching their foul limit (e.g., 4 fouls) has their `foulFactor` reduced (lowering their defensive contribution) and becomes a substitution priority
- **Intentional fouling**: Late-game AI logic triggers intentional fouls when trailing by 1–6 points with little time left — targeting the opposing team's worst free throw shooter
- **Last-two-minutes rule**: Separate foul counter for the final 2 minutes per quarter, preventing early-foul banking

Foul trouble is a major strategic dimension: a star player in early foul trouble forces a difficult decision between sitting them out (losing their contribution) or risking fouling out.

---

### Clock and Pace Management

Base clock consumption per possession is driven by a **pace factor**:
```
clockUsed = basePossessionLength × paceFactor × randomVariance
paceFactor = team[o].compositeRating.pace  // Faster teams = shorter possessions
```

The simulation also models specific late-game clock strategies:

| Situation | Strategy |
|---|---|
| 32–52 seconds left in period | "Two-for-one": offense rushes shot to get another possession before the buzzer |
| ≤26 seconds left in period | "Last shot hold": offense milks clock for the final shot |
| Winning team, final period | "Run out clock": offense drains time to deny opponent possessions |
| Losing team late | Forces faster tempo, increases three-point frequency |

These behaviors emerge from the simulation's probability calculations, not scripted events — the AI reacts to clock and score state every possession.

---

### Box Score Generation

Every possession appends events to a running play-by-play log and updates per-player stat accumulators:

**Per-player tracked stats:**
- `pts` (points), `fgAttempted`, `fgMade`, `tpAttempted`, `tpMade`, `ftAttempted`, `ftMade`
- `orb` (offensive rebounds), `drb` (defensive rebounds), `ast` (assists), `tov` (turnovers)
- `stl` (steals), `blk` (blocks), `ba` (blocks absorbed), `pf` (personal fouls)
- `min` (minutes played), `energy` (final energy level)
- Plus/minus: `+/-` tracked per possession

**Team-level stats:**
- Running score, timeouts remaining, foul counts per quarter

Advanced stats (PER, Win Shares, BPM, VORP) are computed post-simulation from box score totals using known basketball statistics formulas.

---

### Overtime and Special Modes

**Overtime:** Simulated identically to regulation periods but with configurable length (default: 5 minutes) and the same possession-based engine.

**ELAM Ending (optional):** A target-score mode where, once a certain clock threshold is hit, the game ends when any team reaches a target score (current score + N points). This eliminates intentional fouling in late-game situations and is configurable per league.

**Playoffs vs Regular Season:** Fatigue accumulates across games in a series. The `fatigueFactor` constant is higher in the playoffs, meaning fatigue degrades ratings faster — modeling the physical intensity of playoff basketball. Rest days between games allow partial energy recovery.

---

### Player Selection for Individual Actions

Throughout the simulation, when the code needs to pick **which specific player** performs an action (shooter, rebounder, assister, fouler), it uses weighted random selection:

```typescript
const weights = players.map(p => p.compositeRating[type] * p.fatigue ^ power);
const totalWeight = sum(weights);
const r = random() * totalWeight;
// Walk the cumulative weight array to find the selected player
```

The `power` parameter allows some actions to be more concentrated (e.g., star players dominate usage more than their proportional rating difference suggests) or more diffuse (e.g., defensive rotations are shared more evenly).

---

### Performance Architecture

The simulation is designed for speed because thousands of games must simulate per season:

- **Zero UI rendering during simulation**: All computation happens in the background Shared Worker; UI updates only after full game completion (or at explicit play-by-play pause points)
- **No async/await in the inner loop**: The hot path (possession resolution) is fully synchronous — no promises, no waiting
- **Pre-computed lookup tables**: Synergy sigmoid thresholds and other frequently-computed values are cached, not recalculated each possession
- **Team composite ratings updated once per possession** (not per play within a possession), batching the expensive weighted-average calculation

A full 82-game NBA season (each game ~200 possessions) = ~16,400 possessions to simulate. Basketball GM simulates full seasons in ~1 second in modern browsers.

---

### Key Design Principles to Carry Forward Into Soccer GM

From deep study of Basketball GM's simulation engine, these are the principles that matter most:

1. **Composite ratings as simulation inputs, never raw ratings.** Raw attributes must be combined into meaningful composites (e.g., "finishing ability" from pace + strength + technique) before entering probability calculations.

2. **Possession/event-based simulation, not aggregate rolling.** Each discrete event (shot, duel, interception) is individually resolved. This produces realistic per-player stat distributions and authentic play-by-play narratives.

3. **Bounded probabilities.** Every probability is clamped to `[0.001, 0.999]` to prevent division-by-zero and probability collapse edge cases.

4. **Multiplicative fatigue on all ratings, not additive.** Fatigue as a multiplier (rather than subtracted flat amount) preserves relative player differences while uniformly degrading performance — a 90-rated player tired is still better than an 80-rated player fresh.

5. **Performance factor for score differential.** The `tanh`-based penalty for trailing teams (and bonus for leading teams) creates natural game dynamics without scripted "momentum" — large leads are psychologically and tactically self-reinforcing.

6. **Synergy rewards balanced rosters.** Skill combination bonuses incentivize building diverse squads rather than stacking one attribute. This makes roster construction decisions more interesting.

7. **Weighted random selection, not deterministic assignment.** Even in a single possession, variance is preserved — the best player doesn't always shoot; the best rebounder doesn't always grab the board. This produces realistic game-to-game and season-level variance.

8. **Late-game AI strategy must be baked in.** Clock management, intentional fouling, shot selection adjustments, and tempo changes in the final minutes make games feel realistic and strategic. These are not cosmetic — they change outcomes.

---

### How Soccer GM Will Apply These Principles

| Basketball GM Concept | Soccer GM Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Possession (offense vs defense) | Attack phase (team with ball vs defensive shape) |
| Shot types (at-rim / mid-range / 3pt) | Chance types (through-ball through box / shot outside box / header / 1v1) |
| Turnover (steal/bad pass) | Interception / clearance / bad touch → counterattack |
| Offensive rebound | Second ball after save / clearance → retained possession |
| Foul + free throws | Foul → free kick / penalty |
| Player selection weights | Ball carrier / shooter / header target selection weights |
| Team composite ratings per possession | Team attacking/defensive shape rating per phase |
| Fatigue depletion by energy | Stamina depletion by minutes played |
| Pace factor | Pressing intensity / deep-block mentality affects phase length |
| Score-differential performance factor | Same — teams chasing games change tactics, creating feedback loops |
| Synergy (passing + dribbling) | Synergy (pressing + high line, or tiki-taka passing combinations) |
| Intentional fouling late | Time-wasting / parking the bus late |
| Play-by-play log | Match commentary log (goals, shots, cards, near-misses) |
| Box score (pts/reb/ast) | Match stats (goals/shots/passes/tackles/interceptions per player) |
